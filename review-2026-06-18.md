# Review: electron-magnetic-moment

## Verdict
**Accept with major revisions.** The paper's spine — reading the digits of $1.00115965218\ldots$ as historical strata and reframing the number as a metrological instrument that audits the fine-structure constant via the Berkeley–Paris–Fan triangle — is correct, well-sourced, and arithmetically sound where it matters; the experimental anchor values and the bibliography are unusually clean. But Section 5 (the QED series), which is supposed to be the technical heart, contains a wrong three-loop coefficient, a five-loop uncertainty that is ~16× too small, an internally contradictory truncation-uncertainty proof, a flatly false claim that Volkov's independent five-loop check was "consistent," and a misattribution of the dominant mass-dependent term by two loop orders. **The single most serious problem:** the paper claims to "compute every term in the decomposition from published coefficients," but the mass-dependent term is silently fit by difference, and several of the "published coefficients" it does use are wrong or carry the wrong uncertainty — so the section that should be most rigorous is the least reliable.

Attributing the dominance to a four-loop piece is off by two loop orders.

## Argument

- **§1 / §5.2 — the central methodological gap.** The thesis is "the stratigraphic reading is not a metaphor … we compute every term." But one term ($r_{\text{mass-dep}}$, $2.84\times10^{-12}$) is a fitted catch-all, not a computed contribution, and it is dressed up in §5.2 as a physical four-loop quantity. The decomposition is thus partly a tautology: the total equals the measurement *because the residual was set to make it so*. The paper should either compute the mass-dependent term from $A_2^{(4)}, A_2^{(6)}, A_3$ independently, or state plainly that it is a closure residual.
- **§7.2 (Proposition, $\alpha$-limited) — broken proof, surviving conclusion.** The proof asserts the truncation uncertainty is "dominated by $\sigma(c_5)=0.0099$," then computes $\sigma(c_5)(\alpha/\pi)^5 = 6.70\times10^{-16}$ and a total of $2.45\times10^{-14}$ — meaning the total is actually dominated by $\sigma(c_4)=0.00084$ (which contributes $2.445\times10^{-14}$), contradicting the sentence. Worse, both inputs are wrong: post-Laporta (2017), $c_4$ is exact ($\sigma\approx0$), and the real $\sigma(c_5)\approx0.159$ gives $\sigma_{\text{trunc}}\approx1.1\times10^{-14}$. Ironically the qualitative claim ($\sigma$ from $\alpha$ exceeds truncation by ~4–9×) survives with correct numbers, but the proof as written is internally inconsistent and built on superseded uncertainties.
- **§5.1 — outdated as of a May 2026 paper.** The five-loop is described as resting on "the single calculational effort of the Kinoshita group … the principal unverified link." By 2024 Volkov completed an independent *total* tenth-order calculation and the 5σ discrepancy was found and resolved (revised value ~6.80(13)). A 2026 paper should reflect this; instead it uses the 2019 value and the wrong history.
- **§4.2 — the "stratigraphy" reframing is presentation, not result.** The decomposition $a_e^{\text{th}}=\sum c_n(\alpha/\pi)^n + \ldots$ is textbook; calling each term a "stratum" is a nice organizing metaphor but adds no new physics. The genuinely contemporary point (number-as-instrument auditing $\alpha$) is also the field's standard reading, not original here. For a venue expecting insight, the contribution is synthesis, which the paper should own rather than imply discovery.
- **Genuinely strong:** the Cs–Rb–Fan triangle (§7) is the right contemporary story, every number reproduces, and the propagation $\sigma(a_e^{\text{th}})\approx a_e\cdot\sigma(\alpha^{-1})/\alpha^{-1}$ is correctly applied. The historical chain (§2–§3, §6) is accurate and the references support it.

## Factual errors

1. **$c_3 = 1.181\,234\,016\,816$ is wrong** (§5.1, `qed_series.py`). Correct Laporta–Remiddi value: $1.181\,241\,456\,587$. Verified.
2. **$\sigma(c_5) = 0.0099$ is wrong** (`qed_series.py`, §7.2 proof). AKN 2019 give $6.737(159)$; the uncertainty should be ~0.159, ~16× larger. The central value $6.7349$ is also a minor typo for $6.737$. Verified.
3. **"$r_{\text{mass-dep}}$ … dominated by the four-loop $A_2^{(8)}(m_e/m_\mu)$"** (§5.2) is wrong; the dominant mass-dependent term is the **two-loop** $A_2^{(4)}(m_e/m_\mu)$. Verified.
4. **"Volkov (2019) … results consistent at the published precision"** (§5.1) is false; it was a ~5σ discrepancy. Verified.
5. **$\sigma(c_4)=0.00084$** is used (`qed_series.py`) even though the $c_4$ value employed is Laporta's exact (2017) result, for which $\sigma\approx0$. Internal inconsistency.
6. **Proof contradiction** (§7.2): "dominated by $\sigma(c_5)$" while the cited numbers make it $\sigma(c_4)$-dominated.

No errors found in the historical dates/attributions (Stern–Gerlach 1922, Uhlenbeck–Goudsmit 1925, Dirac 1928, Kusch–Foley 1947/48, Schwinger 1948, Penning 1936) or in the experimental table values, which check out.

## Prose and insight

**Alive:** The opening move — "The leading $1$ is Dirac (1928). The digits after the decimal … are everything Dirac's theory missed" — is crisp and earns the stratigraphy conceit. The geonium passage ("Its only environment is the trap, and the trap's perturbations … are calculable from first principles") is genuinely good science writing. "The factor $2$ was an empirical patch" and the *Knabenphysik* aside land well. The §7 pivot ("The constant becomes the bottleneck") is the paper's best idea, cleanly stated: more precise $a_e$ now "buys proportionally less" until a third $\alpha$ measurement breaks the tie.

**Dead:** §8 ("What the decimal does") largely re-lists the abstract and §7; the three "operationally distinct" uses are stated at a level of generality that adds little ("probes any new physics that couples to the electron magnetic moment"). The closing "every layer has a date" is a fine landing but is reached twice.

**Genuine contribution:** A clean, accurate, well-cited synthesis that correctly identifies the present limiting systematic (the Cs–Rb $\alpha$ tension) and dramatizes the seventy-five-year precision chain. It is expository rather than novel — no new physics — and that is fine, but the paper's framing occasionally implies more ("not a metaphor," "we compute every term") than the simulation delivers. The insight is real; the rigor of the QED-internals section does not yet match the rest.
